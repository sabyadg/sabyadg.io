What I Do

Broadly interested in the interplay of geometry, elasticity, and self assembly at SoftBio interfaces. I intend to investigate mechanics of living systems to understand the role of forces, flow and fluctuations within cells to tissue networks. The major interests are how cells shape up in response to a mechanical stimulus; how biomolecules react to sense such biomechanical feedback leading to organizing a coherent cell cytoskeleton; and the role of these self organizational principles in tissue growth and cancer.

Physics of SoftBio Systems

SoftBio Matter are characterized by sizes of few nanometers to micrometers and interaction energies that are comparable to thermal energies. This allows the use of toolboxes comprised of continuum mechanics and statistical physics to explore diverse phenomenon ranging from capillarity, wrinkling, wetting, cell adhesion, tissue growth and cellular transport occurring across various length and time scales. Everything starting from (ice)creams, paints, and jellies to living matter such as cells, tissues and embryos fall under the realm of soft condensed matter. Apart from contributing to fundamental understanding of states of matter responsive to thermal stresses like polymers, membranes, colloidal systems; this field of study has deep implications towards applications such as in discovering novel functional materials, providing technology solutions as well as to understanding physical basis of dieseases.

Research

In my published works, I have studied biomembrane conformations and interface deformations and their interactions with particles in a mesoscopic level treating lipid bilayers / fluid interfaces as 2D soft surfaces. Some general descriptions introducing these works are discussed below with relevant references.

I have aimed at obtaining the rich and complex budding phase diagrams of lipid bilayers interacting with nano-particles. These works have important applications to drug delivery, cellular trafficking of cargo/pathogens via membrane budding during endocytosis and also understanding role of nanotoxicity in cells.

See Ref. [ 1 , 2 , 3 ]

I have investigated cell adhesion to nanostructured interfaces; investigating the role of shape and postion of cylidrical and mushroom shaped nanopillars to achieve efficient coupling of such 3D structures with cells. These optimizations have shown promising advances towards the goal of making multi-electrode arrays as biosensors for measuring stimulus from electrogenic cells.

See Ref. [ 4 ]

I am also investigating self assembly via capillary interactions at fluid interfaces of micron sized anisotropic colloidal particles.

See Ref. [ 5 ]
